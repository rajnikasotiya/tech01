### Question 1: Understand the scenario

Deploying an ML model to optimize energy usage in a smart city introduces three major challenges: data privacy, equity, and cost management. First, collecting real-time energy consumption data from households and businesses may expose sensitive personal information if data security is weak. This can reduce user trust and create legal concerns for stakeholders such as utility providers and local governments. Second, biased ML models may distribute energy resources unfairly across regions, causing underserved communities to receive fewer benefits and negatively affecting social equity. Third, implementing smart sensors, cloud infrastructure, and continuous monitoring systems can create high operational and maintenance costs. These financial challenges may limit long-term scalability and environmental sustainability if resources are not managed efficiently and responsibly.

### Question 2: Analyze the challenges

The first implication is reduced user privacy due to continuous monitoring of household energy usage. Users may feel uncomfortable if their personal consumption patterns are stored or shared insecurely. Stakeholders such as utility companies and governments may also face compliance and cybersecurity risks. The second implication is unfair energy allocation caused by biased ML predictions, which can negatively affect low-income or underserved communities and reduce public trust in the system. This may also create unequal environmental benefits across regions. The third implication is high infrastructure and maintenance costs associated with smart devices, cloud services, and ML model updates. These costs can impact stakeholders financially and increase energy consumption from computing infrastructure, affecting environmental sustainability. Addressing these implications requires secure data governance, fairness-aware AI models, and cost-efficient deployment strategies.

---

### Question 3: Propose solutions

To protect user privacy, the smart city should implement differential privacy, encryption, and role-based access control so sensitive energy data remains secure while still supporting accurate ML predictions. To address equity concerns, fairness-aware AI models and regular bias audits should be introduced to ensure equal energy optimization benefits across all demographic groups and regions. Transparent reporting and stakeholder reviews can further improve accountability and public trust. For cost management, cloud-based auto-scaling, edge computing, and energy-efficient infrastructure should be used to reduce unnecessary operational expenses while maintaining performance. These solutions align with ethical AI, responsible data governance, and cost-benefit analysis concepts discussed in the course. Together, these approaches create a scalable, fair, secure, and environmentally sustainable ML system for smart city energy optimization.
